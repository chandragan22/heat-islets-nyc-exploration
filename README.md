# heat-islets-nyc-exploration
Exploring Dr. Anamika Shreevastava's paper "Emergent self-similarity and scaling properties of fractal intra-urban heat islets for diverse global cities"

## Introduction
This project explores the fractal nature of heat islets in New York City using the framework introduced by Shreevastava in her 2019 Physical Review E Paper. I used the USGS Landsat 8 Collection 2 Level-2 surface temperature data for a single cloud-free scene over NYC on June 11 2025, processed to mask water bodies and crop to borough boundaries. Applying thermal thresholds at the 70th, 85th, and 95th percentiles, I identified heat islet clusters and computed their size distributions. I found approximate power law behavior with scaling exponents of 0.51 to 0.59 across thresholds, lower than Shreevastava's universal value of 1.88, which may be consistent with analyzing a single city at non-percolation thresholds rather than at the critical point where fractal scaling is most cleanly expressed.

## Data and Methods
The code is written in Python using numpy, matplotlib, geopandas, rasterio, and scipy as the primary Python libraries. To process the data, I applied a NYC boundary shapefile to the USGS Landsat 8 Collection 2 Level-2 surface temperature data for June 11 2025, converted raw pixel values to Celsius using the standard Collection 2 scale factor, and applied the QA pixel band to mask water bodies and isolate land surface temperatures.

I then computed heat islet clusters at the 70th, 85th, and 95th percentile thermal thresholds using connected component labeling, and plotted the cluster size versus rank on a log-log scale to test whether the size distributions follows a power law consistent with fractal scaling. To compare against Shreevastava's universal scaling exponent, I then fit a linear regression to the log-log distribution at each threshold and extracte the scaling exponent \beta. 

## Results and Takeaways
The cluster size versus rank plots reveal approximately linear behavior on log-log axes for clusters between 10 and 1000 pixels in size, consistent with power law scaling. At higher percentiles the distribution becomes more spread and noisy as clusters become sparser and smaller. 
Fitting the power law yielded scaling exponents of β = 0.51 at the 70th percentile, 0.55 at the 85th, and 0.59 at the 95th, all significantly below Shreevastava's universal value of 1.88 ± 0.23. Converting from rank-size slope to probability density function exponent by adding 1 gives values of 1.51 to 1.59, which are closer but still below the universal value. The R² values of approximately 0.90 indicate approximate but imperfect power law behavior, expected at non-percolation thresholds. Shreevastava's exponent is an average across 78 diverse global cities at the critical percolation threshold, analyzing a single city at arbitrary percentile thresholds naturally produces deviation from the universal value. An interesting extension would be testing how the scaling exponent changes across seasons or during an extreme heat event, when clusters may approach the percolation threshold more closely.
